What is Claude + Cowork?

Claude is an AI assistant — think of it like a very capable colleague who never gets tired, is always available, and can turn a rough idea into a finished document in seconds. It lives inside the Claude desktop app, which is installed on your computer just like Microsoft Word or Outlook.

Inside the desktop app there are three different tabs — Chat, Cowork, and Code. Think of them as three different modes for working with Claude. For the Bishop's House team, Cowork is the one you'll use for almost everything in this guide.

The Three Tabs — What Each One Does

When you open the Claude desktop app, you'll see these three tabs across the top. Here's what each one is for:

How to Open the Cowork Tab
Step by step — getting to Cowork
1. Find the Claude desktop app on your computer. Look for the Claude icon in your taskbar at the bottom of the screen, or in the Start menu. Double-click to open it.

2. The app opens. Along the top you will see three tabs: Chat, Cowork, and Code. Click Cowork.

3. The Cowork panel opens. This is where everything in this guide takes place.

4. Click in the message box at the bottom of the screen, type what you need, and press Enter.

If you can't find the Claude desktop app on your computer, contact Marti — it may need to be installed first. This is a five-minute job.
What it looks like in practice

The Chat tab looks and feels like a simple messaging app — just a text box and replies. The Cowork tab has the same text box but also shows your connected apps (Outlook, OneDrive) and your output folder where finished documents are saved. You'll see a difference immediately the first time you use it.

4. Microsoft 365 Integration

The Claude desktop app connects directly to your Diocese of Carlisle Microsoft account — giving it access to your Outlook email, OneDrive files, SharePoint documents, and calendar. This is what turns Claude from a helpful assistant into a genuine part of your daily workflow.

💡 What does "Microsoft 365 integration" actually mean?

Think of it this way: normally, to find an old email you'd have to remember who sent it, scroll back through your inbox, or guess at search terms. With M365 connected, you just ask Claude in plain English — "Can you find any emails from Canon X about the property matter in autumn?" — and it does the searching for you, returning a summary of what it found.

The same applies to OneDrive files: Claude can open a Word document you've saved, read it, summarise it, and use its contents when drafting something new — without you having to copy and paste anything. For SharePoint, it can find and read shared documents across the Diocese in the same way.

Your Outlook calendar can also be read by Claude — so you can ask "What does the Bishop's diary look like next week?" and get a clear summary, or ask it to help draft a meeting invitation based on what you describe.

📧 Outlook
Search emails, summarise threads, draft replies — Claude works through your inbox so you don't have to.
📁 OneDrive & SharePoint
Open, read, and summarise Word documents, PDFs, and files — without copying and pasting content in.
📅 Calendar
Ask what's in the diary, check availability, or draft meeting invitations based on what you describe.
🔒 You stay in control
Claude can read and draft — but it cannot send emails, delete files, or make any changes without you explicitly asking it to.

🛠️ How to Set Up a Scheduled Task — Step by Step

Setting up a scheduled task requires no technical knowledge at all. You do the whole thing in plain English, directly in the Cowork chat window. Here's how, using the weekly diary briefing as a worked example.

1
Open Cowork
Launch the Claude desktop app as normal. You don't need to go to any special menu or settings page.
2
Tell Claude you want to create a scheduled task
Just type this to get started:
"I'd like to set up a scheduled task."
Claude will confirm it's ready and ask you to describe what you'd like it to do.
3
Describe what you want it to do
Explain the task in plain English — as you would to a new member of staff. The more detail you give, the more useful the output will be.
"Every Monday morning, check Bishop Rob's Outlook calendar for the coming week. For each appointment, prepare a short briefing note: who he is meeting, where, and anything relevant I should know in advance. Save it as a Word document in my OneDrive."
4
Set the schedule
Tell Claude when you'd like it to run. You can be as specific or as simple as you like.
"Run this every Monday at 8am."
Claude handles all the scheduling automatically — you don't need to set any timers or alarms.
5
Claude shows you a summary — confirm to save
Claude will repeat back what it will do and when. Check it looks right, then type "Yes, save that" — and the task is set up. From that point on, it runs automatically every Monday morning without anyone needing to do anything.
6
Find the output when it's ready
When the task runs, Claude saves the output to your Cowork folder. You'll see it waiting there when you arrive in the morning. You can also run any scheduled task immediately on demand by asking: "Run the Monday diary briefing now."

Privacy, Data & Confidentiality

This is one of the most important questions for any organisation handling sensitive information — and it deserves a straight answer, not just reassurance. There are two separate things to understand: what Anthropic (the company that makes Claude) does with your data, and what the Diocese is responsible for under UK data protection law.

🔒 What the Claude Team Licence Guarantees

The Diocese holds a Claude for Work (Team) licence, which is governed by Anthropic's Commercial Terms — not the consumer terms that apply to free or personal accounts. This matters significantly. The commercial terms include the following hard guarantees:

🚫
Your data is never used to train AI
Anthropic's commercial terms state explicitly: "Anthropic may not train models on Customer Content." What the Bishop's team types into Claude stays within the Diocese's contract — it does not feed back into the AI or anyone else's experience.
📄
A Data Processing Addendum is in place
The commercial licence includes a formal Data Processing Addendum (DPA) — the legal agreement required under UK GDPR when an organisation shares data with a third-party processor. This means the relationship is legally defined and contractually binding.
🏛️
The Diocese owns everything
All inputs (what you type) and all outputs (what Claude produces) belong to the Diocese. Anthropic makes no claim to the content of your conversations, documents, or drafts.
👤
Strict limits on who at Anthropic can see anything
Anthropic employees can only access data on a strict need-to-know basis, and only those bound by their own confidentiality obligations. This is the same model used by reputable legal, financial, and IT service providers.
✅
In short: The contractual protections are strong. The commercial licence was specifically designed for organisations handling confidential information — and it was the right choice for the Diocese.
⚖️ The GDPR Distinction — What the Diocese is Still Responsible For

The commercial terms protect the Diocese's data within Anthropic's systems. But under UK GDPR, the Diocese remains the data controller for anything it puts into Claude. This is not a gap in the licence — it is simply how data protection law works. The same principle applies to any third-party service the Diocese uses: a solicitor, an accountant, a cloud storage provider.

What this means in practice is that staff need to apply the same judgement they would with any trusted external professional. Share what is needed to get the task done — no more. The ICO (the UK's data protection regulator) is clear that organisations using AI tools are responsible for what personal data they input, and must have a lawful basis for processing it.

🚦 A Practical Guide for Staff — What to Put In, and What Not To

Think of this as a simple traffic light. Most day-to-day tasks are straightforwardly fine. A small number of situations need more care.

🟢  Generally fine to use Claude for
• Drafting letters, emails, and communications
• Summarising meeting notes or agendas
• Creating templates and standard documents
• Financial reports and budget analysis
• Diary management and scheduling
• Researching topics, policies, or guidance
• Producing service orders, rotas, or programmes
• Formatting, proofreading, and editing text
🟡  Use with care — think before you paste
Personal details about named individuals (parishioners, clergy, staff) — if you are drafting a letter about a specific person or referencing their circumstances, Claude can help, but consider whether you need to include their full name and address, or whether initials or "a parishioner" would do the same job.

Personnel or HR matters — general HR letter templates are fine. Drafting something about a specific named employee's conduct or personal situation needs more thought. Where possible, anonymise before pasting in.
🔴  Do not paste into Claude
Safeguarding case notes or referrals — these contain special category data under UK GDPR Article 9 and must remain within the Diocese's own secure systems. Claude can help you produce templates or blank forms, but never paste in real case details.

Detailed medical, health, or pastoral confidences about identifiable individuals — the same principle applies. Claude is not the right tool for processing this kind of information.

Financial account credentials, passwords, or login details — Claude does not need these and should never be given them.
A note for Marti (IT): Where Claude is used systematically to process personal data — for example, a scheduled task that regularly handles staff or parishioner information — the ICO recommends completing a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA). This does not have to be a lengthy exercise, but it creates a documented record of what data is processed, why, and what safeguards are in place. It would be worth raising with whoever holds the Diocese's data protection responsibility (the DPO or equivalent) to confirm whether one is needed for any particular workflow.

Questions about a specific task? If Ali or a member of the admin team is unsure whether something is appropriate to use Claude for, the rule of thumb is: would I be comfortable sharing this with a trusted external professional to help draft or summarise it? If yes, Claude is likely fine. If it feels sensitive enough that you'd normally keep it entirely in-house, keep it in-house with Claude too. Marti is happy to advise on specific cases.
